Abstract

The physiological effects of prolonged weightlessness represent a critical bottleneck constraining deep-space exploration. Existing artificial gravity solutions (rotating centrifuge modules, linear acceleration) suffer from structural bulk, high energy consumption, and poor physiological adaptation. This paper proposes a novel artificial gravity paradigm—the Dignity Field—which utilizes carefully engineered magnetic field gradients to generate directional body stress within human tissue, simulating the physiological effects of Earth gravity.

This solution employs a compact 2 \times 2 meter cabin module, optimized for seated/squatting physiological postures, utilizing high-pressure scandium-based superconducting loops to generate approximately 50 Tesla-class gradient magnetic fields, achieving 0.5g equivalent gravity within a 10.5-ton total mass constraint. The system employs flexible high-permeability gel to sculpt field topology, biocompatible coatings to ensure safety, and the Holographic Memory Formalism (HMF) algorithm for intelligent control. Theoretical analysis indicates that this system can satisfy astronauts' daily requirements for essential gravity exposure (toilet use, showering, short-duration rest) while maintaining launch feasibility aboard existing Long March 5 / Falcon Heavy-class heavy-lift vehicles.

Keywords: Artificial gravity; Magnetic field gradient; Body stress; Scandium-based superconductor; Holographic Memory Formalism; Compact life support

1. Introduction

International Space Station data demonstrates that astronauts in microgravity environments experience bone density loss of 1–2% per month, with muscle atrophy, cephalad fluid shifts, and vestibular dysfunction severely constraining long-duration missions. Future Mars missions (approximately 3 years round-trip) without effective artificial gravity intervention would subject the human body to irreversible damage.

Existing solutions exhibit distinct limitations: rotating centrifuge modules require large radii (>30 m) to avoid Coriolis effects, presenting structural complexity and launch difficulties; linear acceleration requires continuous propellant consumption; localized wearable devices cannot simulate whole-body physiological effects. Consequently, exploring an engineering-feasible, mass-controllable, physiologically compatible artificial gravity solution has become an urgent requirement in the field of human spaceflight.

This paper proposes the Dignity Field—a compact artificial gravity paradigm based on gradient magnetic fields. The core concept resides in the following: the essence of gravity sensation is body stress. By generating directional body stress within human tissue via non-contact magnetic fields, and optimizing for seated posture—wherein gravitational loading concentrates on the pelvis and lower lumbar spine, precisely the regions most susceptible to bone density loss in microgravity environments.

2. Dignity Field Physical Principles: Seated Posture Optimization Paradigm

2.1 Body Stress Model and Seated Posture Adaptation

The force exerted on the human body in a gravitational field can be expressed as:

\vec{F}(\vec{r}) = \rho(\vec{r}) \cdot \vec{g}(\vec{r})

In the Dignity Field, magnetic gradient forces substitute for gravitational attraction:

\vec{F}m(\vec{r}) = \nabla (\vec{m}(\vec{r}) \cdot \vec{B}(\vec{r}))

where \vec{m}(\vec{r}) is the equivalent magnetic moment of human tissue (primarily generated by the diamagnetism of water molecules), and \vec{B}(\vec{r}) is the applied magnetic field.

Key Optimization—Seated Posture Paradigm: This solution optimizes for seated/squatting posture (height H = 1.2 m) rather than standing. This design is based on the following physiological rationale:

- Bone density loss primarily occurs in the pelvis and lumbar spine; seated posture precisely compresses these load-bearing regions;
- Excretory difficulties in microgravity stem from the absence of intra-abdominal pressure; seated posture restores Earth-like abdominal pressure;
- Static seated posture minimizes stimulation to the vestibular system, reducing maladaptation risks.

These considerations directly determine subsequent gradient field parameter design—the objective shifts from covering full-body standing height to focusing on achieving effective physiological loading within a 1.2-meter seated height range.

2.2 Gradient Field Design

The magnetic field strength distribution along the vertical direction z (feet at z=0, seated buttocks at z=H) follows:

B(z) = B_0 \cdot e^{-\lambda z}

where \lambda = \frac{\ln 2}{2H} \approx 0.289\ \text{m}^{-1}, ensuring that the force at the buttock region equals 50% of that at the feet, creating a natural weight distribution—that is, the "weight" perceived at the hips is approximately half that at the feet, consistent with Earth-based seated force patterns.

The body stress (force per unit volume) is:

\sigma(z) = \frac{|\chi|}{\mu_0} \cdot B(z) \cdot \frac{dB}{dz} = \frac{|\chi|}{\mu_0} \cdot \lambda \cdot B_0^2 \cdot e^{-2\lambda z}

where \chi \approx -9\times10^{-6} (volume magnetic susceptibility of water-based tissue), and \mu_0 = 4\pi\times10^{-7}\ \text{H/m}.

The target equivalent gravitational acceleration is g{\text{eq}} = 0.5g (Martian gravity, sufficient to maintain physiological function). Substituting the average human density \rho \approx 1000\ \text{kg/m}^3, and solving from \sigma{\text{avg}} = \rho g{\text{eq}}:

B_0 = \sqrt{\frac{\rho g{\text{eq}} \mu_0}{|\chi| \lambda}} \approx 48.7\ \text{Tesla}

This field strength falls within the achievable range of current superconducting technology (theoretical limits of scandium-based hydride superconductors >100 T), and similar magnitude field maintenance has been achieved under laboratory high-pressure conditions.

2.3 Outward Compliance and Inward Compression

- Outward Compliance: The human body as a whole experiences magnetic gradient forces with constant direction (pointing toward the floor), creating an "up-down" perception. As the force acts on every water molecule, it possesses global characteristics without requiring wearable equipment.
- Inward Compression: Various tissues within the body generate microscopic stress distributions due to differential magnetic susceptibility, particularly pronounced in seated posture—the pelvic region bears maximum stress, simulating natural loading on the skeletal system during Earth-based sitting, thereby specifically counteracting bone density loss.

5.1 HMF Mathematical Formalism

HMF consists of state evolution equations and memory update equations:

State Evolution:

B{t+1}(\vec{r}) = B_t(\vec{r}) + \eta(\vec{r}) \cdot \left[ -K(\vec{r})(B_t(\vec{r}) - S(\vec{r})) + \xi_t(\vec{r}) \right] + \alpha \cdot \Gamma_t(\vec{r})

where B_t is the magnetic field distribution, S is the target field pattern (0.5g corresponding distribution), \xi_t is disturbance, and \Gamma_t is the holographic memory term.

Memory Update (Exponential Moving Average Form):

\Gamma{t+1}(\vec{r}) = \alpha \cdot \Gamma_t(\vec{r}) + (1-\alpha) \cdot \Delta B{t+1}(\vec{r})

\alpha \in (0.5, 0.95) is the memory depth coefficient, ensuring smooth decay of historical information. This form preserves the core memory mechanism of HMF while providing more concise numerical implementation.
